This model maps what a comprehensive Year 1 SRE budget looks like for a school opening for the first time, and shows how three distinct funding streams cover each spending category.

The goal is to inform how CSGF thinks about the right investment approach across the EIR general grant and MAP/enrollment grant — and to ground that conversation in realistic numbers.

During the pilot, CSGF invested $60k per EIR in MAP support — approximately $40k toward planning, infrastructure, and top-of-funnel (including $20k in paid media), with the balance toward consulting and campaign management.

Several EIRs still did not hit their enrollment goals.

The core question: Was the shortfall a digital underspend problem — or a whole-budget problem, with critical categories like dedicated FTE, field strategy, and post-offer yield work going unfunded?

This model suggests it was largely the latter. MAP covered the top of the funnel. The bottom half — and the human capacity to work it — was often underfunded or absent.

The MAP/enrollment grant is not designed to cover the entirety of an EIR's SRE budget. It is scoped to digital campaigns, paid media, and Mariposa consulting support.

A comprehensive Year 1 strategy requires all three funding streams. MAP covers the digital infrastructure. The EIR general grant is the pre-revenue bridge that makes everything else possible.

The single largest line item is dedicated FTE capacity (25–35% of total SRE spend) — and the most frequently underfunded.

Lead conversion, post-offer nurture, offer management, registration follow-through, and summer onboarding are all FTE labor. There are no separate lines for these. If the FTE is missing, the back half of the funnel goes unworked — regardless of how strong the paid media campaign is.

Benchmark: ~1.3 FTEs per 100 newly enrolled students (2024 SRE Study). Collegiate Academies hired their school leader a year early, spending ~50% of their time on SRE — their best investment.

Before Day 1, there is no per-pupil revenue. The EIR general grant is the bridge that makes a dedicated enrollment hire fundable.

"Enrollment can't be 30% of someone's job and succeed."

Recommendation: 20–25% of the general grant earmarked for SRE, with MAP layered on top, is the floor for a comprehensive strategy at 100 students.
  • What % of the general grant should be earmarked for SRE? At what point does CSGF set expectations with EIRs that a minimum allocation is needed to run a viable strategy?
  • What is MAP meant to accomplish within that broader strategy? Are EIRs clear that MAP covers the digital layer — not the whole budget?
  • How do we set expectations before an EIR applies? EIRs that treat MAP as their full enrollment strategy will underinvest in FTE and field work — and likely miss targets regardless of ad performance.
  • Should SRE allocation be a structured part of EIR onboarding? This model could be a tool in that conversation: showing founders the full picture and the decisions they need to make from the general grant.
